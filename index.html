Why Highway 395?

Recommended by Grandpa — a man who knows every bend of this road. The Eastern Sierra is one of America's great drives: desert floor at 3,700 ft in Lone Pine, sub-alpine lakes at 9,000 ft above Mammoth, ancient trees, a ghost town, an alien lake, and then Yosemite's back door. No interstate. No franchises. Just the Sierra Nevada on your left, the White Mountains on your right, and the road ahead.

The Route

Highway 395 Stops

Every stop earns its place

1
Red Rock Canyon State Park
Hwy 14, Mojave
Nature
›
What it is

Striking red and white cliffs rising from the Mojave Desert floor — a dramatic shift from the LA basin. The canyon walls glow orange in the afternoon light and the rock formations feel genuinely alien.

What to do

Pull off Hwy 14 and walk among the formations — no hiking required. The visitor center has a short trail. This is a 20–30 minute stop, not a half-day. Just enough to stretch legs and feel the desert begin.

Practical

Free entry. Open 24 hrs. Gas up before entering the Mojave stretch — next reliable stop is Lone Pine. Cell service fades here.

2
Alabama Hills
Lone Pine · 2 nights base
Nature / Film
›
What it is

A labyrinth of rounded granite boulders and golden arches at the foot of Mt. Whitney — the tallest peak in the lower 48. The contrast is staggering: warm desert rock in the foreground, 14,505 ft of snow-capped Sierra behind. Over 400 films have been shot here, including Gladiator, Star Trek, Django, and countless westerns.

What to do

Drive Movie Road through the boulders — paved, no 4WD needed. Walk to Möbius Arch (easy 1-mi round trip): it perfectly frames Whitney at sunrise. Drive up Whitney Portal Road for canyon views. Museum of Western Film History in town is worth 45 minutes. Stargazing here is extraordinary — zero light pollution.

Practical

Free BLM land — dispersed camping allowed. Ask Grandpa for his favorite spot. Alabama Hills Cafe & Bakery in town: legendary breakfast, massive portions, don't skip the cookies. Lone Pine is the last place for reasonable gas prices heading north — fill up here.

3
Manzanar National Historic Site
10 mi north of Lone Pine
History
›
What it is

One of ten WWII incarceration camps where over 10,000 Japanese-American citizens were imprisoned by the U.S. government — most of them American citizens. Located in the open Owens Valley with the Sierra Nevada towering above, the setting makes the injustice feel even more stark and surreal.

What to do

Walk the reconstructed barracks and mess hall. The interpretive museum is exceptional and deeply moving — voices of former prisoners tell their own stories. A 3.5-mile driving loop covers the grounds. Ranger-led programs on weekends. Allow 1–2 hours. Lorenzo and Gia at 17 and 15 are exactly the right age for this.

Practical

Free entry. Open Mon, Fri, Sat, Sun 9am–4:30pm. Closed Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday — plan the visit for Thursday Jul 10 accordingly. America the Beautiful pass covers entry.

4
Erick Schat's Bakkery
763 N Main St, Bishop
Food
›
What it is

The most famous bakery in California, period. A Dutch family institution since 1938 in the heart of Bishop. The line often wraps around the building, but it moves fast. Every serious 395 road tripper stops here — it's a rite of passage.

What to order

Sheepherder's bread is the signature — a round, crusty sourdough loaf that's been the same recipe for 80+ years. Get the roast turkey sandwich on sheepherder's bread. Stock up on cookies and walnut-raisin baguettes for the road. The deli counter has great road food for the Mammoth stretch ahead.

Practical

Open Mon–Thu 6am–6pm, Fri 6am–7pm, Sat–Sun 6am–6pm. The line looks long but moves quickly. Budget 20–30 minutes. Bishop is also the last major grocery store before Mammoth — good time to stock up on snacks and camp food.

5
Ancient Bristlecone Pine Forest
25 mi east of Big Pine · 10,000 ft elevation
Nature
›
What it is

Home to the oldest living trees on Earth — Bristlecone Pines up to 5,000 years old. Methuselah, the oldest known living tree at ~4,855 years, is somewhere in this grove (its exact location is kept secret to protect it). These trees were old when the Egyptian pyramids were being built. Nothing puts human scale in perspective quite like standing next to them.

What to do

Drive the scenic road up from Big Pine (paved, 45 min from 395). Start at the visitor center for a short film and context. The Discovery Trail is a 1-mile loop through the oldest trees — easy walking. The Methuselah Trail (4.5 miles, more strenuous at altitude) takes you through the most ancient grove. Allow 2.5–3 hours total including the drive.

Practical

Visitor center open 9am–5pm daily. Free entry. Road is paved but steep and winding — fine for the SUV. Bring layers: it's 20–30°F cooler than the valley floor. The altitude (10,000 ft) means even a short walk feels slightly harder — take it slow. Worth every minute.

6
June Lake Loop
12-mile side road off 395 · just north of Mammoth
Nature
›
What it is

A 12-mile loop through four stunningly blue alpine lakes — June, Gull, Silver, and Grant — with the volcanic Mono Craters rising behind them. In July, the water is crystal-clear and warm enough to swim. The small town of June Lake sits on the lakeshore with a sandy beach. One of the loveliest short detours on all of 395.

What to do

Drive the loop and stop at June Lake beach for a swim — the sandy shoreline is one of the best in the Sierra. Rent a kayak or paddleboard from the June Lake Marina. The loop takes about 45 minutes to drive; allow 2 hours if you stop to swim. It connects back to 395 at both ends so no backtracking.

Practical

Free to drive and access beaches. Small town has a general store, a couple of restaurants, and gas (though pricey). Do this loop on the morning of the Mammoth day — water is glassy early and crowds build by noon in July.

7
Devils Postpile National Monument
Mammoth Lakes area
Geology
›
What it is

A wall of 60-foot hexagonal basalt columns, formed when a river of molten lava cooled and cracked into near-perfect geometric shapes. You don't have to go to Iceland to see this — it's right here, and the columns here are among the finest examples in the world. A short walk leads to Rainbow Falls, a 101-foot waterfall where sunlight creates rainbows in the spray.

What to do

Take the mandatory shuttle from the Mammoth Mountain Adventure Center (required in summer — no private cars to the monument during peak hours). Walk to the Postpile (0.5 mi easy), then continue to Rainbow Falls (2 more miles). The top of the Postpile offers a view of the perfectly tiled column tops, like a cracked mosaic. Allow 3 hours total.

Practical

Shuttle runs 7am–7pm daily in summer, departs from Mammoth Mountain Adventure Center. ~$15/adult shuttle fee. Covered by America the Beautiful pass for entry. Open 9am–5pm. Bring water — the trail to Rainbow Falls gets warm.

8
Hot Creek Geological Site
Hot Creek Hatchery Rd, near Mammoth
Geology
›
What it is

A dramatic gorge where geothermal hot springs bubble up directly into the cold, fast-moving Hot Creek — creating turquoise, steaming pools that glow against the volcanic rock. The canyon walls are just 20 feet apart and the contrast between the boiling springs and the icy creek is visually stunning. Swimming is prohibited (water is scalding near the vents), but the views are extraordinary.

What to do

Easy 0.5-mile walk down into the gorge. Go in late afternoon for golden-hour light on the steam — the colors are incredible. The gorge also has excellent trout fishing in the calmer stretches. Go right at sunset if scheduling allows: the light turns the steam columns amber and rose.

Practical

Free entry. Last mile of road is unpaved but fine for SUV. Open 8am–7pm. Takes 45 minutes to an hour. Combine with Minaret Vista on the same afternoon for the best end to a Mammoth day.

9
Minaret Vista
Lookout Point Rd, above Mammoth Mountain
Nature
›
What it is

A viewpoint above Mammoth Mountain looking directly at the Minarets — a row of dramatic, jagged volcanic spires rising from the Sierra crest. The panorama also takes in the Ritter Range, Banner Peak, and on clear days, a sweep of the entire Eastern Sierra. At 9,175 ft, the air is thin and the sky is a deeper blue than anything at sea level.

What to do

Drive up (15 min from Mammoth village) and walk the short loop along the ridge. Go at sunset for the most dramatic light on the Minarets — they glow red and orange before the sky goes dark. Stay for stargazing: this is one of the best dark-sky spots in California, and the Milky Way is fully visible from the parking area. Bring warm layers — it gets cold fast after sunset at altitude.

Practical

Open 24 hours. Free. Pit toilets in the parking lot. The road is paved and the lot is large. Lorenzo and Gia will not forget the Milky Way from this spot.

10
Mono Lake Tufa Reserve
South Tufa area, Lee Vining
Geology
›
What it is

An ancient inland sea three times saltier than the ocean, ringed by ghost-white limestone towers called tufa — formed where underwater freshwater springs met the alkaline lake water over thousands of years. As the lake level dropped, the towers emerged. The landscape is genuinely unlike anything else in California. Eerie, serene, otherworldly.

What to do

Walk the South Tufa Trail (easy 1-mile loop) among the towers right at the water's edge. Book the Mono Lake Committee canoe tour (Saturday mornings, summer only — monolaketours.org) to paddle over the forming tufas underwater. The committee's visitor center in Lee Vining is also worth 30 minutes for the lake's ecological history.

Practical

$3/person entry for South Tufa area. Canoe tour: ~$25/person, books ahead fast — reserve before the trip. Best light is early morning or sunset. The lake's high salinity means you float effortlessly in a canoe — strange and fun. Cell service is spotty in Lee Vining; download offline maps before arriving.

11
Whoa Nellie Deli
22 Vista Point Dr, Lee Vining (Tioga Gas Mart)
Food
›
What it is

A gourmet deli operating inside a Mobil gas station on the edge of Lee Vining. This sounds like a joke. It is not. The Whoa Nellie has been called the best food between LA and San Francisco, and it's inside a gas station next to the windshield wiper fluid. This is exactly the kind of thing that makes 395 trips legendary.

What to order

The fish tacos are the signature — enormous, fresh, with a mango salsa that people drive two hours specifically to eat. Breakfast burritos. The BBQ plates. Fresh-baked goods. Eat outside on the picnic benches with views straight down to Mono Lake — one of the better dining views in California.

Practical

Open 7am–8pm daily. Gets crowded at lunch — go early or after 2pm. Also sells camping supplies and souvenirs. Fuel prices here are high; fill up in Mammoth. Gas station is the last one before the long climb over Tioga Pass into Yosemite.

12
Bodie State Historic Park
13 mi east of 395 near Bridgeport
Ghost Town
›
What it is

The largest unrestored ghost town in the American West. At its peak in 1879, Bodie had 10,000 residents, 65 saloons, and a murder rate that made it notorious even by Gold Rush standards. Today, 200+ buildings remain exactly as they were abandoned — furniture, calendars, medicine bottles, rusting cars left in place. The state maintains it in "arrested decay."

What to do

Wander the deserted streets freely — peer through windows at still-set tables, dusty beds, open books. Book the Standard Mill tour (the ore-processing facility), the only interior you can enter with a guide. Walk the cemetery on the hill above town. Allow at least 2.5 hours. Lorenzo and Gia will be genuinely riveted — it's viscerally eerie in the best way.

Practical

Opens 9am, closes 4pm — plan accordingly. Leave Mono Lake by 11:30am at the latest to do both properly. Last 3 miles of road are unpaved but easily passable in the SUV. Entry: ~$8/adult. No food or fuel at Bodie — eat at Whoa Nellie Deli first. Mill tour tickets sell out — buy at the museum when you arrive.

13
Travertine Hot Springs
South of Bridgeport · free BLM land
Hot Springs
›
What it is

A series of natural hot spring pools on open BLM land just south of Bridgeport, with unobstructed views of the Eastern Sierra. No infrastructure, no fees, no reservations — just concrete pools filled by natural geothermal water at various temperatures, set against a mountain backdrop that looks like a painting.

What to do

Soak after a long day hiking Bodie and the tufa towers. Multiple pools at different temperatures — find the one that's right. The upper pools tend to be hottest. Go at sunset or at night for the full effect: the Milky Way overhead, the Sierra silhouetted, and hot water on tired legs after five days on 395. Ask Grandpa which pool he recommends — he'll know.

Practical

Free. No reservation. Short walk from the dirt parking area. Bring towels and water shoes (rocky bottom in some pools). Can be busy on weekends — go in the evening for more solitude. Good free BLM campsites nearby if camping overnight before the Tioga Pass drive.

14
Tioga Pass · Tuolumne Meadows
Yosemite · 9,945 ft — highest drivable pass in California
Nature
›
What it is

The eastern entrance to Yosemite National Park via Tioga Road — one of the great mountain drives in North America. The road climbs from the desert floor at Lee Vining to nearly 10,000 ft through glacially sculpted granite domes, alpine meadows, and sapphire lakes. Tuolumne Meadows at the summit is a vast high-altitude grassland surrounded by peaks — serene, open, and nothing like Yosemite Valley below.

What to do

Stop at Olmsted Point for the first view down into Yosemite Valley — Half Dome appears on the horizon. Walk the easy Soda Springs trail in Tuolumne Meadows (1 mi, flat) through the meadow to the natural carbonated springs. If time allows, continue down into Yosemite Valley for Half Dome views from the valley floor. Then drive west (Hwy 120) to the Bay Area — about 3.5 hours from the valley.

Practical

Timed-entry reservation required for Yosemite in summer — reserve on recreation.gov on June 30 for July 14 entry. America the Beautiful pass covers the $35 vehicle fee. Tioga Pass is only open in summer (typically late May through November). Confirm road status at nps.gov/yose as July approaches. Gas up in Lee Vining — no fuel inside the park.
